Ms. Johnson: Correct, Sarah. Oxygen is released into the atmosphere. This process is crucial for life on Earth.
Billy: What happens at night?
Ms. Johnson: Great question. At night, plants respire, which is similar to how we breathe. They take in oxygen and release carbon dioxide, although they produce much more oxygen during the day.
Sarah: So plants breathe like us?
Ms. Johnson: In a way, yes. During the day, photosynthesis dominates, and plants release oxygen. At night, only respiration occurs, so they take in small amounts of oxygen. But overall, plants produce far more oxygen than they consume.
Maria: Does this happen in all plants?
Ms. Johnson: Yes, all green plants perform photosynthesis because they contain chlorophyll, the green pigment that captures sunlight. Even algae and some bacteria do it too.
Billy: What about cacti in the desert? They don't have many leaves.
Ms. Johnson: Excellent observation! Cacti have adapted to conserve water. They perform a special type of photosynthesis called CAM, where they open their pores at night to reduce water loss. They store carbon dioxide and use it during the day.
Sarah: That's really cool!
Ms. Johnson: It is! Nature has amazing adaptations. Now, let's look at the equation for photosynthesis. Who can tell me what goes in?
Maria: Water and carbon dioxide?
Ms. Johnson: Yes, and what else?
Billy: Sunlight!
Ms. Johnson: Perfect. And what comes out?
Sarah: Glucose and oxygen.
Ms. Johnson: Exactly right. The plant uses glucose for energy and growth, and releases oxygen as a byproduct. This is why forests are often called the lungs of the Earth.
Maria: How much oxygen does one tree produce?
Ms. Johnson: A mature tree can produce enough oxygen for about two people per year. That's why preserving forests is so important for our planet.
