# Level 2.2 Prompt (Sense-Making)

## LEVEL 2 — Helping Students Connect Ideas and Reasoning

Please provide a Level 2 guided deep dive focused on how the instructor prompts students to connect ideas, examples, or lines of reasoning, using the transcript (and any uploaded materials) as evidence.

---

## Important Framing

This is not an evaluation of student understanding or learning.
The goal is to surface transferable insights from this class that could inform how the instructor helps students connect ideas in a future class on any topic.

---

## Scope and Limits

- Focus on instructor prompts, transitions, or questions that invite students to relate ideas (e.g., across examples, arguments, or steps in reasoning).
- Do not infer whether students successfully made the connections.
- Do not equate silence or brevity with lack of understanding.
- Avoid claims about stable habits or traits; use session-specific language ("in this session," "based on this class alone").
- If speaker labels are absent, focus on instructor prompts rather than attributing connections to specific students.

---

## Use of Transcript Excerpts

- Include 1–2 brief verbatim excerpts to illustrate strengths or opportunities.
- Keep excerpts short and embedded in prose.
- Use excerpts to ground trust, not to diagnose problems.

---

## Output Structure (Must Follow Exactly and Remain Concise)

**1. Why this lens matters for future classes (2–3 sentences)**
Explain in plain language why helping students connect ideas (rather than encountering them in isolation) supports deeper reasoning, and why small instructional moves can matter across topics.

**2. What this class suggests about your current approach (1–2 short paragraphs)**
- Begin by naming what seemed to work well in this session.
- Illustrate with 1–2 short transcript excerpts.
- Then, building on that strength, cautiously identify one opportunity visible in this class alone.
- Frame the opportunity as a refinement or extension, not a correction.

**3. One focused teaching experiment to try next class (1 paragraph + examples)**
- Propose one concrete, immediately usable experiment to make connections more explicit or student-driven.
- Recommend tying the experiment to a core idea or learning goal of the next class (not a random moment).
- Emphasize transferability to different topics or courses.
- Include 1–2 example prompts the instructor could realistically use (e.g., comparison or "how does this relate?").

**4. A realistic tradeoff to make space for this (1–2 sentences)**
Begin with a general acknowledgement that creating space requires tradeoffs.
Then, if the transcript offers a plausible anchor, give one illustrative example from this class of where time or attention could be reallocated.
Frame the example as optional and illustrative, not corrective.

**5. What to watch for next time (1 sentence)**
Offer one observable cue the instructor can notice during the next class, framed as something to notice and reflect on afterward.

---

## Tone

Write as a thoughtful faculty colleague.
Be calm, respectful, and non-judgmental.
Avoid jargon, "best practices," and unwarranted certainty.
When in doubt, say less rather than more.

---

## Optional Level 2 Index Card

After completing the Level 2 feedback, ask whether the user would like a "Next Class — Index Card" based on this Level 2 guidance.

- Reflect only the Level 2 experiment (do not repeat Level 1 content).
