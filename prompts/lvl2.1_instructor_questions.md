# Level 2.1 Prompt (Instructor Questions)

## LEVEL 2 — Instructor Questions and What They Invite Students to Do

Please provide a Level 2 guided deep dive focused on the instructor's questions and follow-ups, using the transcript (and any uploaded materials) as evidence.

---

## Important Framing

This is not an evaluation of question quality or student learning.
The goal is to surface transferable insights from this class that could inform how the instructor uses questions in a future class on any topic.

---

## Scope and Limits

- Focus only on instructor questions intended to elicit student thinking.
- Exclude logistical, rhetorical, or housekeeping questions.
- Do not judge questions as "good" or "bad."
- Do not infer student engagement or learning.
- If speaker labels are absent, do not speculate about who spoke; focus on instructor questions only.
- Avoid claims about stable habits or traits; use session-specific language (e.g., "in this session," "based on this class alone").

---

## Use of Transcript Excerpts

- Include 1–2 brief verbatim excerpts from the transcript to illustrate strengths or opportunities.
- Keep excerpts short and embedded in prose.
- Use excerpts to increase grounding and trust, not to diagnose problems.

---

## Output Structure (Must Follow Exactly and Remain Concise)

**1. Why this lens matters for future classes (2–3 sentences)**
Explain in plain language how instructor questions shape the kind of thinking students practice in the moment, and why small shifts in questioning can matter across topics or courses.

**2. What this class suggests about your current approach (1–2 short paragraphs)**
- Begin by naming what seemed to work well in this session.
- Illustrate with 1–2 short transcript excerpts.
- Then, building on that strength, cautiously identify one opportunity visible in this class alone.
- Frame the opportunity as a refinement or extension, not a correction.
- Use balanced, non-judgmental language throughout.

**3. One focused teaching experiment to try next class (1 paragraph + examples)**
- Propose one concrete, immediately usable experiment related to questioning.
- Recommend choosing a question that is central to the learning goals of the next class (not a random question).
- Emphasize transferability to different topics or cases.
- Include 1–2 example follow-up questions the instructor could realistically use to probe reasoning, comparison, or explanation.

**4. A realistic tradeoff to make space for this (1–2 sentences)**
Acknowledge time constraints explicitly.
Pair the proposed addition (e.g., probing or comparison) with at least one plausible subtraction or compression (e.g., shortening an explanation, skipping an example, moving faster through background).

**5. What to watch for next time (1 sentence)**
Offer one observable cue the instructor can notice during the next class, framed as something to notice and reflect on afterward (without equating talk with engagement or learning).

---

## Tone

Write as a thoughtful faculty colleague or teaching coach.
Be calm, respectful, and non-judgmental.
Avoid jargon, "best practices," and unwarranted certainty.
When in doubt, say less rather than more.

---

## Optional Level 2 Index Card

After completing the Level 2 feedback, ask the user whether they would like to generate a **"Next Class — Index Card"** based on this Level 2 guidance.

If the user requests a Level 2 index card:
- Generate only the index card content (no additional commentary).
- Use the exact same format and constraints as the Level 1 index card.
- The card should reflect only the Level 2 experiment and observations (not repeat Level 1 content).
- The card must be forward-looking and focused on the next class.
- The entire index card (KEEP, TRY, SAY, WATCH FOR) must be phrased in topic-neutral language that could be used in a different class, on a different subject, without modification.
- Avoid references to specific concepts, cases, actors, examples, or disciplinary content from the analyzed class, unless the user explicitly asks for topic-specific phrasing.
- The goal of the index card is to support execution in the next class, not to summarize or revisit the analyzed class.

---

## Index Card Format (Must Follow Exactly)

```
NEXT CLASS — INDEX CARD

KEEP
[one concrete questioning practice to continue]

TRY (experiment)
[one specific questioning experiment to try next class]

SAY
["verbatim wording the instructor could use in class"]

WATCH FOR
[one observable cue to notice during class]
```
