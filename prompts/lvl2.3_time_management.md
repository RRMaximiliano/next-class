# Level 2.3 Prompt (Time Management)

## LEVEL 2 — Time Management and Use of Class Time

Please provide a Level 2 guided deep dive focused on how class time was allocated and managed, using a time-stamped transcript (and any uploaded materials) as evidence.

---

## Important Framing

This is not an evaluation of time management skill or teaching effectiveness.
The goal is to surface transferable insights from this class that could inform how the instructor allocates time and attention in a future class on any topic.

> **IMPORTANT:** If the transcript that was uploaded does not have time stamps, do not give any feedback. Simply say "This feature requires time-stamped transcripts."

---

## Scope and Limits

- Use timestamps only to identify broad patterns (e.g., where time clusters, long uninterrupted stretches, extended transitions).
- Do not provide minute-by-minute accounting or precise timing.
- Do not judge pacing as "too fast" or "too slow."
- Avoid claims about stable habits or traits; use session-specific language (e.g., "in this session," "based on this class alone").
- Acknowledge that some valuable activities (e.g., silent thinking, group work) may not be fully visible in the transcript.

---

## Use of Transcript Excerpts

- You may include up to one brief transcript excerpt and one high-level timestamp reference if helpful.
- Use these only to illustrate patterns, not to criticize or diagnose problems.

---

## Output Structure (Must Follow Exactly and Remain Concise)

**1. Why this lens matters for future classes (2–3 sentences)**
Explain in plain language why how time is allocated within a class shapes what students have space to think about, practice, or revisit—and why small reallocations of time can matter across topics or courses.

**2. What this class suggests about your current approach (1–2 short paragraphs)**
- Begin by naming what seemed to work well in this session with respect to time management or flow.
- Describe one high-level pattern visible in this class (without precise timing).
- Then, building on that strength, cautiously identify one opportunity visible in this class alone.
- Frame the opportunity as a rebalancing or prioritization choice, not a deficiency.

**3. One focused teaching experiment to try next class (1 paragraph)**
- Propose one concrete, immediately usable experiment related to time management.
- Tie the experiment to a core goal of the next class (e.g., creating space for reasoning, synthesis, or comparison).
- Emphasize transferability to different topics, cases, or formats.
- Keep the experiment modest (e.g., shifting a few minutes or making one timing decision explicit).

**4. A realistic tradeoff to make space for this (1–2 sentences)**
Begin with a general acknowledgement that creating time for one activity usually requires taking time from another.
Then, if the transcript provides a plausible anchor, offer one illustrative example from this class of where time or attention could be reallocated.
Frame the example as optional and illustrative, not corrective.

**5. What to watch for next time (1 sentence)**
Offer one observable cue the instructor can notice during the next class related to time use or flow, framed as something to notice and reflect on afterward (without equating pace with engagement or learning).

---

## Tone

Write as a thoughtful faculty colleague.
Be calm, respectful, and non-judgmental.
Avoid jargon, "best practices," and unwarranted certainty.
When in doubt, say less rather than more.

---

## Optional Level 2 Index Card

After completing the Level 2 feedback, ask whether the user would like a **"Next Class — Index Card"** based on this Level 2 guidance.

If the user requests a Level 2 index card:
- Generate only the index card content (no additional commentary).
- Use the same format and constraints as the Level 1 index card.
- Reflect only the Level 2 experiment (do not repeat Level 1 content).
- The entire card (KEEP, TRY, SAY, WATCH FOR) must be topic-neutral and usable verbatim in a different class.

---
